# Presentation structure — Spin Sr Product Designer Challenge

Compact deck for a 20-minute presentation.

Spanish version: [`estructura-presentacion-spin.md`](./estructura-presentacion-spin.md)

## Audience

Head of Design, Sr Design Leads, and Head of Product.

Secondary audience: multidisciplinary panel evaluating strategic judgment, execution ability, squad collaboration, and design quality.

## Decision / Expected outcome

Demonstrate a strategic designer stance on whether to advance with this MVP, what solution to propose, how to co-create it with the squad, and how to reduce risk without slowing delivery.

## Governing thought

The challenge is not only to design a credit flow, but to lead the responsible launch of BNPL embedded in OXXO online grocery checkout (priority pilot retailer for partnership ease) that improves conversion and frequency without sacrificing comprehension, trust, or on-time repayment.

## Slide 0: Executive summary — advance with an OXXO-first pilot to learn without compromising trust or credit health

**Recommendation:** Launch a controlled BNPL MVP in OXXO online grocery checkout (`order now, pay in 15 days`) with clear guardrails and explicit criteria to scale, adjust, or stop.

| Dimension | Decision |
| --- | --- |
| **Validate** | Profiles, shopping missions, break moments, current alternatives, and trust language — confirm or refute business assumptions before locking the solution |
| **Design** | 11-stage journey + clarity, control, security, and continuity principles; happy path that proves comprehension and repayment from v1 |
| **Execute** | Agentic design to explore and synthesize; co-creation with PM + PD + Tech Lead triad; parallel discovery and delivery with clear freeze points |
| **Measure** | Adoption, default rate, margin, and take rate — not only the `3,000 credits` target |

Spin can use credit as a responsible learning bet, not as a conversion push without guardrails or evidence.

Learn fast / Protect trust / Decide with evidence.

## Slide 4: I understood the business opportunity as the foundation of the process, not as a brief to illustrate

Before designing flows, I mapped why it makes sense for Spin to advance with BNPL in online grocery checkout and what the MVP must prove.

- **Problem:** checkout abandonment when the user already decided to buy but cannot or does not want to pay the full basket today.
- **Fit:** financing pantry or replenishment in a purchase context is more legitimate than a generic credit line.
- **Leverage:** conversion, repeat purchase, and frequency — with controlled payment risk, not only originating `3,000 credits`.
- **Business decision:** does a 15-day BNPL embedded in checkout make sense, and under what conditions is it worth scaling?
- **OXXO-first pilot:** partnership chosen for integration ease (checkout, data, repayment), not TAM alone.

**View business opportunity:** [`business-opportunity-bnpl.md`](./business-opportunity-bnpl.md)

## Slide 5: The first design decision is knowing which business, risk, user, technology, and operations assumptions to validate

Before locking the solution, I would clarify the fronts that determine whether the MVP is desirable, viable, understandable, and operable.

- **Business:** beyond `3,000 credits`, which outcome matters most: conversion, basket size, repeat purchase, learning, or margin?
- **Retail partner:** what checkout, data, and repayment agreements with OXXO are minimum to launch in 4 months?
- **Risk/legal:** what eligibility, disclosure, delinquency, and collections rules apply?
- **User:** for which purchase types does paying later feel legitimate?
- **Tech/data:** what has backend already assumed and what signals exist for eligibility, tracking, and monitoring?
- **CX/operations:** how will payment be reminded, what happens if it fails, and what must support resolve?

## Slide 6: The 11 journey stages show where BNPL converts without sacrificing trust

The journey shows when budget pressure hits and what the user needs to decide.

**Customer Journey:**

| Stage | Situation | Need / question | Primary risk | Design opportunity |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Plans the purchase | Prepares pantry or replenishment | "What do I need to buy today?" | Basket too large for budget | Separate necessary vs discretionary missions |
| 2. Builds the cart | Adds products and adjusts quantities | "Can I afford everything?" | Friction from price, promos, or substitution | Cart progress and less friction before payment |
| 3. Feels budget pressure | Reaches checkout and doubts paying in full today | "Pay in full, trim, or leave it?" | Abandonment or basket reduction | Activate BNPL only when it makes sense |
| 4. Discovers BNPL option | Sees "pay in 15 days" at checkout | "Does this work for me? Is it trustworthy?" | Rejection due to debt or distrust | Simple benefit, no hype |
| 5. Reviews eligibility and terms | Checks amount, cost, total, date, and late fees | "How much do I pay later and when?" | Poor comprehension or later surprise | Mandatory financial summary |
| 6. Decides to accept | Compares BNPL with card, promo, or trimming | "Does this help me close without getting into trouble?" | Impulsive acceptance or fear-based rejection | Responsible friction and visible exact due date |
| 7. Confirms order | Completes checkout and gets confirmation | "Is it done? What do I do next?" | Ambiguity about active debt | Clear order and repayment confirmation |
| 8. Receives the order | Purchase arrives and value materializes | "Was it worth using this option?" | Poor delivery experience hurts BNPL perception | Reinforce post-purchase control, don't push more credit |
| 9. Receives reminders | Due date approaches | "When is it due and how do I pay?" | Delinquency from forgetfulness or low visibility | Useful, not aggressive reminders |
| 10. Pays | Settles via Spin, OXXO cashier, or other channel | "How do I close this easily and with certainty?" | Operational failure or unapplied payment | Simple flow in known ecosystem; immediate receipt |
| 11. Repeats or is restricted | System allows a new purchase or blocks for lateness | "Can I use it again?" | Risky reuse or opaque rules | Visible continuity or pause rules |

**Moments of truth (callouts in the visual):**

- Budget shock at checkout (stage 3)
- Financial summary (stage 5)
- Order confirmation (stage 7)
- Reminder before due date (stage 9)
- Second use (stage 11)

## Slide 7: My design principles make the financial decision legible before pushing conversion or irresponsible use of financing

These principles define the experience so the user understands, accepts, and pays without surprises — clarity and control over visual urgency.

**Design principles**

| Principle | What we design |
| --- | --- |
| Clarity | Financial summary with readable hierarchy: amount, total cost, exact date, and late consequence visible before acceptance |
| Control over urgency | Sober microcopy and layout; no dark patterns, countdowns, or pressure that undermines a conscious decision |
| Security | OTP, identity, and confirmation framed as protection; deliberate steps where risk requires it |
| Continuity | Same navigation, tone, and retail checkout patterns; the user does not "leave" for another product |

---

## Slide 8: BNPL wins at checkout when budget tightens, not as generic credit

Value appears at journey stage 3: the user already built the cart, wants to buy, but does not want or cannot pay in full today.

- Legitimate use case: finance necessary household purchases (pantry, replenishment), not impulsive discretionary spending.
- Easy benefit to explain: get groceries today and pay in 15 days.
- Repayment via Spin and/or OXXO cashier reinforces trust and operations within a known ecosystem.
- Design activates BNPL only when context justifies it — not as a permanent offer on every checkout.

---

## Slide 9: The happy path must prove the user understands, accepts, and can pay with confidence

The flow must not only originate credit; it must validate comprehension, consent, and payment from the first version.

- **Checkout offer:** visible option for eligible users: "get it today and pay in 15 days".
- **Financial summary:** purchase amount, service cost if applicable, total due, and due date.
- **Confirmation:** explicit acceptance of the commitment.
- **Order confirmation:** reinforcement of amount owed, when to pay, and where to check status.
- **Reminder and repayment:** clear reminders and simple payment via Spin, OXXO cashier, or agreed channel.

---

## Slide 10: The squad needs a decision system, not another handoff process

To move fast without losing quality, design must help product, engineering, research, content, data, and risk decide together.

- Activate a strong triad: `PM + Product Designer + Tech Lead`.
- Content Designer and UX Researcher join from framing, comprehension, and validation.
- Data defines baseline, events, cohorts, and thresholds from day one.
- Legal, risk, CX, and **OXXO partnership stakeholders** review the solution before final lock.
- Use simple rituals: triad twice a week, definition review, and a living decision log.
- Every important decision should clarify what changed, who decided, the impact, and implications for development.

## Slide 11: A 4-month MVP requires parallel discovery and delivery with clear freeze points

Execution must allow learning without blocking development, using progressive definitions and explicit decisions.

**Project plan:**

- **Weeks 1–2: Strategic framing:** align real objective, retail partner criteria, risks, baseline, critical questions, and existing backend assumptions.
- **Weeks 3–5: Lightweight discovery:** validate JTBD, use-case legitimacy, comprehension, trust, and preliminary happy path.
- **Weeks 6–8: Solution shaping:** translate findings into rules, states, content, components, and code-first prototype.
- **Weeks 9–12: Build and launch readiness:** refine QA, analytics, support, exit criteria, and pilot preparation.

**Key milestones for healthy execution:**
- Close a **preliminary happy path** by end of week 3.
- Close **experience principles + minimal architecture + reusable components** no later than week 5.
- Reach week 7 with the flow fully defined, not merely sketched.
- Use weeks 8–10 for fine iteration, not to discover the main solution.

**Presenter note:** The plan does not wait for perfection; it advances with progressive decisions and freeze points.

---

## Slide 12: Success means healthy adoption, clear comprehension, and evidence to scale or stop

The MVP should only be considered successful if it shows value for business and user without compromising model health.

- **Adoption:** percentage of eligible checkouts that use BNPL.
- **Understanding:** % of users who correctly explain amount, date, and late consequence (post-checkout survey).
- **Contribution margin:** financial contribution of the product to the business.
- **Default rate:** % of credits with delinquency > X days within acceptable thresholds.
- **Take rate:** % of GMV originated via BNPL that generates net commission.
- **Support:** volume of tickets due to lack of clarity.
